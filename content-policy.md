Turn your content site from a generic publishing operation into an evidence-backed specialist publication.

## What Google actually confirms

Google explicitly says:

- E-E-A-T is not one direct ranking factor.
- Quality raters do not directly control rankings.
- Clear bylines and links to author information are strongly encouraged.
- AI-assisted content is allowed; using automation primarily to manipulate rankings is not.
- First-hand, non-commodity content is more valuable than summaries of existing information.
- Normal SEO still applies to AI search.
- You do not need special AI schema, `llms.txt`, artificial “content chunking,” or manufactured mentions.

These points are documented in Google’s [people-first content guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content), [generative-AI optimization guide](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/ai-optimization-guide), and [AI-generated content policy](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2023/02/google-search-and-ai-content).

## What I recommend for your website

### 1. Establish a narrow area of authority

Choose three to five subject areas where you have actual knowledge, data, customers, projects, or testing experience.

For each area:

- Create one definitive hub or guide.
- Connect supporting articles to it with descriptive internal links.
- Cover natural follow-up questions without creating dozens of near-duplicate pages.
- Remove unrelated topics that make the site’s purpose difficult to understand.

Google confirms that AI search uses “query fan-out”—multiple related searches behind one question—but warns against publishing a separate thin page for every possible variation.

### 2. Build a real authorship system

Every editorial article should have a named individual author. Avoid “Admin,” “Editorial Staff,” or a generic company byline unless the organization genuinely authored the material collectively.

Each article should show:

- Author’s full name and real photograph.
- Role and relevant subject expertise.
- Link to a permanent author page.
- Published and genuinely modified dates.
- Expert reviewer when the topic is medical, financial, legal, security-related, or otherwise sensitive.

Each author page should contain:

- A specific, factual biography.
- Relevant experience, credentials and certifications.
- Topics the person is qualified to cover.
- Selected work, projects, publications or speaking appearances.
- Verifiable external profiles.
- An index of their articles.
- A way to contact or verify the organization.

Do not invent credentials or pad biographies with vague claims.

### 3. Upgrade articles with first-hand evidence

For every important article, ask: “What can we add that another writer or AI cannot obtain simply by reading existing search results?”

Good evidence includes:

- Original screenshots and process photographs.
- Actual test results and measurements.
- Tools, versions and settings used.
- Before-and-after outcomes.
- Mistakes, failed approaches and limitations.
- Case studies with timelines and methodology.
- Interviews with identifiable subject-matter experts.
- Original surveys or anonymized first-party data.

If you have no experience with a subject, interview someone who does or reconsider publishing it. Do not manufacture first-person language.

### 4. Create a clearer article template

Your standard article structure should be:

1. A descriptive, non-clickbait title.
2. A direct answer or useful summary near the top.
3. Who the guidance is intended for.
4. Author, reviewer and accurate dates.
5. First-hand context or methodology.
6. Clearly labelled steps, findings or recommendations.
7. Original evidence and properly linked sources.
8. Caveats, exceptions and limitations.
9. Relevant next steps and internal links.
10. A concise author card.

Remove generic introductions, repeated conclusions, filler FAQs, excessive affiliate equipment lists and multiple images that show essentially the same thing.

This organization is for readers—not artificial “AI chunking.” Google specifically says there is no ideal page length and no requirement to break everything into tiny sections.

### 5. Audit existing content before publishing more

Export at least 12–16 months of Google Search Console data and prioritize:

- Pages ranking around positions 4–20.
- Pages with high impressions but poor clicks.
- Formerly successful pages showing meaningful decay.
- Commercially or strategically important pages.
- Pages competing against one another for the same intent.
- Articles lacking authorship or original evidence.

Assign each page one action:

- Keep.
- Substantially improve.
- Merge and redirect.
- Reposition for a different intent.
- Remove only when it is unsalvageable.

Changing the date or rewriting sentences without adding value is not a meaningful update. Google also advises that deletion should be a last resort.

### 6. Implement accurate technical and structured data

On articles, use `Article`, `BlogPosting`, or `NewsArticle` schema containing truthful visible information:

- `headline`
- `image`
- `datePublished`
- `dateModified`
- `author` as `Person`
- `author.url` pointing to the author page
- `publisher`
- `mainEntityOfPage`

Use `ProfilePage` with a `Person` entity on author pages and add genuine `sameAs` external profiles. Google’s [Article schema documentation](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/article) and [ProfilePage documentation](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/profile-page) explicitly support these connections.

Also verify:

- Important pages return HTTP 200 and are indexable.
- Canonicals and XML sitemaps are correct.
- Googlebot is not unintentionally blocked.
- Mobile pages are readable and responsive.
- Main content is not buried beneath ads, popups or sticky elements.
- Schema matches what visitors can actually see.
- No SEO plugin is producing duplicate or conflicting schema.

Schema helps Google understand content and enables search features; it does not create authority by itself.

### 7. Add publication-level trust pages

Create or improve:

- About the publication.
- Contact page.
- Editorial and fact-checking policy.
- Corrections policy.
- Content update policy.
- AI-assistance policy.
- Ownership and funding disclosure when relevant.
- Privacy and terms pages.

If AI is used substantially, explain its role where that information would help readers. Keep a qualified human accountable for every published claim; do not list an AI system as the author.

### 8. Build authority and traffic outside Google

Use search as discovery, not your entire business model.

Select one or two sustainable direct channels:

- Email newsletter.
- YouTube.
- An industry community.
- A podcast.
- A relevant social platform.

Pursue legitimate external recognition through expert contributions, interviews, professional associations, conference participation, independent reviews and genuinely newsworthy research. Avoid paid “mentions,” fake profiles and low-quality guest-post schemes.

## A practical 90-day sequence

**Days 1–14:** Export Search Console data, inventory content, identify topic overlap, audit author attribution, indexing, schema and page friction.

**Days 15–30:** Build author pages, publication policies and improved article templates. Correct Article/ProfilePage schema and technical problems.

**Days 31–60:** Upgrade the 10–20 strongest opportunity pages with clearer answers, first-hand evidence, better sourcing and internal links. Merge overlapping weak pages.

**Days 61–90:** Publish one or two flagship assets—an original case study, experiment, benchmark or research report—and distribute them through email and relevant third-party channels.

Track impressions, clicks, conversions, branded searches, returning visitors, newsletter growth and Google’s Generative AI performance report. Treat third-party “AI visibility scores” as directional estimates, not Google ranking data.
